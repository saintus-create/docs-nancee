# Cicero and Quintilian

> "The ideal orator is the good man skilled in speaking." — Quintilian

## Cicero (106-43 BC)

Marcus Tullius Cicero was Rome's greatest orator and a foundational figure in rhetorical theory.

### Major Works

1. **De Oratore** (On the Orator)
   - Three dialogues on the ideal orator
   - Combines philosophy with practice

2. **Brutus**
   - History of Roman oratory
   - Portrait of the ideal orator

3. **Orator**
   - Theory of the perfect style
   - Three styles: plain, middle, grand

### Contributions

- **United Greek and Roman rhetoric** - Adapted Greek theory to Roman context
- **Orator-statesman ideal** - Eloquence in service of public good
- **Latin rhetorical vocabulary** - Created enduring terminology
- **Philosophical synthesis** - Combined Academic skepticism with rhetoric

### Political Speeches
- **Catiline Orations** - Against conspirator Catiline
- **Philippics** - Against Mark Antony

## Quintilian (c. 35-100 AD)

Marcus Fabius Quintilian wrote the *Institutio Oratoria*, the most comprehensive surviving treatise on rhetorical education.

### Major Work

**Institutio Oratoria** (The Education of the Orator)
- 12 books covering complete rhetorical education
- From elementary to advanced training

### Key Concepts

1. **"The good man skilled in speaking"** (vir bonus dicendi peritus)
   - Moral character essential to true eloquence

2. **Rhetorical Education**
   - Sequential training
   - Progressive complexity
   - Practice and imitation

3. **Triple Criterion of Oratory**
   - To teach (docere)
   - To move (movere)
   - To delight (delectare)

## Comparison

| Aspect | Cicero | Quintilian |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Style | Grand, ornamental | Balanced, moderate |
| Focus | Orator as statesman | Orator as professional |
| Method | Philosophical dialogue | Systematic treatise |
| Tone | Elegant, sophisticated | Practical, educational |

## Legacy

Both Cicero and Quintilian shaped:
- Renaissance humanist education
- Legal training
- Political oratory
- Literary criticism
