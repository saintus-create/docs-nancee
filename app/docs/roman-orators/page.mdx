# Roman Orators

> "Roman rhetoric adapted Greek theory to Roman needs." — James M. May

## Overview

Roman orators developed distinctive approaches to rhetoric, emphasizing practical eloquence in legal, political, and ceremonial contexts.

## Major Roman Rhetoricians

### 1. Marcus Tullius Cicero (106-43 BC)
The greatest Roman orator and theorist.

**Major Works:**
- *De Oratore* (On the Orator)
- *Brutus* (History of Roman Orators)
- *Orator*

**Contributions:**
- United Greek theory with Roman practice
- Developed the ideal of the orator-statesman
- Established Latin rhetorical vocabulary

**Philosophical Works:**
- *De Republica*
- *De Officiis*

### 2. Quintilian (c. 35-100 AD)
Author of the *Institutio Oratoria* (The Education of the Orator).

**Major Work:** 12-volume comprehensive treatise on rhetorical education

**Contributions:**
- Systematic approach to rhetorical training
- Emphasis on moral character of the orator
- "The good man speaking well"

### 3. Seneca the Elder (c. 54 BC - 39 AD)
- *Controversiae* (School debates)
- *Suasoriae* (Persuasive speeches)

### 4. The Rhetorica ad Herennium
Traditional attribution to Cicero, but likely by an unknown author.

**Contents:**
- Four books on rhetoric
- Comprehensive treatment of invention, arrangement, style, memory, delivery

## Roman Oratorical Contexts

### 1. Senate Speeches
- Deliberative rhetoric
- Political debate

### 2. Courtroom Speeches
- Forensic rhetoric
- Criminal and civil cases

### 3. Ceremonial Oratory
- Epideictic rhetoric
- Funerals, dedications

## The Orator Ideal

The Roman ideal of the orator:
- Excellent speaking ability
- Broad education
- Political wisdom
- Moral character

## Legacy

Roman rhetoric influenced:
- Renaissance humanism
- Legal education
- Political oratory
- Literary theory
