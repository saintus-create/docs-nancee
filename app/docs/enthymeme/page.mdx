# The Enthymeme

> "The enthymeme is the rhetorical syllogism." — Aristotle

## Overview

The enthymeme is Aristotle's term for the rhetorical syllogism—a form of reasoning adapted for persuasion rather than strict logical demonstration.

## Definition

An enthymeme is an argument in which:
- One premise (or the conclusion) is left unstated
- The missing element is supplied by the audience or context
- It relies on probabilities and signs (not certainties)

## Structure

### Complete Syllogism (Logical)
```
Major premise: All men are mortal.
Minor premise: Socrates is a man.
Conclusion: Therefore, Socrates is mortal.
```

### Enthymeme (Rhetorical)
```
Major premise: All men are mortal.
Conclusion: Therefore, Socrates is mortal.
(Implicit: Socrates is a man)
```

## Types of Enthymemes

### 1. From Signs
Reasoning from observable indicators to underlying facts.

**Example:** "She speaks well; therefore, she is educated."

### 2. From Probabilities
Reasoning from what usually happens to what happened.

**Example:** "It rained yesterday, so the ground will be wet today."

### 3. From Example
Using specific instances to support a general claim.

**Example:** "Socrates was unjustly executed; therefore, democracy can be unjust."

## Role in Rhetoric

### Ethos, Pathos, and Enthymeme

- **Ethos-based** - Character as premise
- **Pathos-based** - Emotional appeal as premise
- **Logos-based** - Logical structure

### Practical Advantages

1. **Efficiency** - Don't state the obvious
2. **Engagement** - Audiences "fill in" the blanks
3. **Appropriateness** - Matches everyday reasoning
4. **Persuasiveness** - More effective than formal logic

## Contemporary Applications

- Legal argumentation
- Political rhetoric
- Advertising
- Everyday persuasion

## Criticism

Some scholars argue enthymemes are:
- Too vague for rigorous analysis
- Merely truncated syllogisms
- Difficult to identify clearly
