# Informal Fallacies

> "A fallacy is an argument that appears to be valid but is not." — C.L. Hamblin

## Overview

Informal fallacies are errors in reasoning that arise from the content or context of an argument rather than from its logical structure. Unlike formal fallacies, which violate the rules of formal logic, informal fallacies involve ambiguous or misleading language, unwarranted assumptions, or irrelevant appeals.

## Common Fallacies

### Ad Hominem (الحجة على الشخص)
Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.

**Types:**
- **Abusive** - Direct personal attacks
- **Circumstantial** - Attacking based on circumstances
- **Tu Quoque** - "You too" - pointing to the accuser's behavior

**Example:** "You can't trust his opinion on tax policy—he's never held a real job."

### Ad Populum (الاستAppeal to the Masses)
Appealing to popular opinion or the beliefs of the majority as evidence.

**Example:** "Everyone knows that supplements are safe, so they must work."

### Straw Man (رجل القش)
Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack.

**Example:** "You think we should have no borders, so you want open borders for everyone!"

### False Dilemma (المعضلة الزائفة)
Presenting only two options when more exist.

**Example:** "You're either with us or against us."

### Slippery Slope (المنحدر الزلق)
Arguing that one event will lead to a chain of negative events without evidence.

**Example:** "If we allow students to redo assignments, soon they'll expect to redo everything, and eventually no one will do any work at all!"

### Circular Reasoning (الت reasoning الدائري)
Using the conclusion as a premise.

**Example:** "The Bible is true because it's the word of God, and we know it's God's word because the Bible says so."

### Hasty Generalization (التعميم المتسرع)
Making broad conclusions from limited examples.

**Example:** "I met two rude people from that city—everyone there must be rude."

### Red Herring (السمك المذيل)
Introducing irrelevant material to distract from the issue.

**Example:** "Why should we discuss the budget deficit? What about the children?"

### Appeal to Authority (الاست Appeal to Authority)
Using an unqualified authority or irrelevant authority as evidence.

**Example:** "This celebrity says this diet works, so it must be effective."

### Post Hoc (بعد ذلك، إذن بسبب ذلك)
Assuming that because one event followed another, the first caused the second.

**Example:** "I wore my lucky socks and we won. My socks caused the victory."

## Classical Origins

The systematic study of fallacies began with Aristotle's *Sophistical Refutations* and was continued by later logicians including C.L. Hamblin's influential *Fallacies* (1970).
