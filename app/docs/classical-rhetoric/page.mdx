# Classical Rhetoric

> "Rhetoric is the art of speaking well." — Plato

## Overview

Classical rhetoric encompasses the rhetorical traditions of ancient Greece and Rome, establishing the foundational principles and practices that continue to influence communication and persuasion today.

## The Five Canons of Rhetoric

### 1. Invention (الاختراع)
The discovery and arrangement of arguments and material appropriate to the rhetorical situation.

### 2. Arrangement (التنظيم)
The organization of material into a coherent structure, including introduction, narration, confirmation, refutation, and conclusion.

### 3. Style (الأسلوب)
The adaptation of language and expression to suit the subject, audience, and occasion.

### 4. Memory (الحفظ)
The techniques for remembering and recalling speeches and arguments.

### 5. Delivery (الإلقاء)
The vocal and physical delivery of a speech, including gesture, posture, and tone.

## Three Branches of Rhetoric

1. **Deliberative (الإرشادي)** - Concerned with future action, advising what should or should not be done
2. **Forensic (الفضائحي)** - Concerned with past events, judging right and wrong
3. **Epideictic (التحريضي)** - Concerned with present circumstances, praising or blaming

## The Sophists

The Sophists were itinerant teachers in ancient Greece who taught rhetoric and were known for their ability to make the weaker argument the stronger one (Plato's critique).

### Key Sophists
- **Protagoras** - "Man is the measure of all things"
- **Gorgias** - Master of epideictic oratory
- **Isocrates** - Founder of systematic rhetorical education

## Greek Philosophy and Rhetoric

### Plato's Critique
Plato criticized Sophistic rhetoric as mere flattery (kolakeia) lacking true knowledge (episteme).

### Aristotle's Defense
Aristotle's *Rhetoric* established rhetoric as a systematic discipline based on logic, ethics, and emotion (ethos, pathos, logos).

## The Attic Orators

The ten canonical Attic orators of classical Athens:
- Antiphon (480-411 BC)
- Andocides (440-390 BC)
- Lysias (445-380 BC)
- Isaeus (420-348 BC)
- Isocrates (436-338 BC)
- Aeschines (389-314 BC)
- Demosthenes (384-322 BC)
- Hyperides (390-322 BC)
- Lycurgus (390-324 BC)
- Dinarchus (361-291 BC)
