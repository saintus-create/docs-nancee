# Medieval Rhetoric

> "Rhetoric is the knowledge of how to arrange ideas and clothe them in language." — Martianus Capella

## Overview

Medieval rhetoric encompasses the rhetorical traditions of the European Middle Ages (c. 500-1500 CE), building upon classical sources while adapting them to Christian, feudal, and emerging civic contexts.

## Key Developments

### 1. Ars Dictaminis (فن الخطابة)
The art of letter-writing that became a fundamental discipline in medieval education.

**Key Features:**
- Formulary collections (dictamina)
- Attention to style and structure
- Medieval administrative communication

**Major Figures:**
- Alberic of Monte Cassino (c. 1000-1080)
- Adalbert of Mainz
- Guido Faba (c. 1190-1244)

### 2. Ars Praedicandi (فن الوعظ)
The art of preaching, combining classical rhetoric with Christian theology.

**Structure of Medieval Sermons:**
1. Thematext (biblical passage)
2. Divisio (division of theme)
3. Amplificatio (elaboration)
4. Conclusio (conclusion)

**Major Preachers:**
- Augustine of Hippo
- Bernard of Clairvaux
- Thomas Aquinas

### 3. Progymnasmata (تمارين Preparatory)
Rhetorical exercises adapted from classical models for medieval education.

## Classical Sources in Medieval Rhetoric

- **Cicero** - De Inventione, De Oratore
- **Quintilian** - Institutio Oratoria
- **Ad Herennium** - Rhetorica ad Herennium
- **Augustine** - De Doctrina Christiana

## The Trivium

Rhetoric formed the third part of the medieval liberal arts curriculum:

1. **Grammar** - Correct language use
2. **Logic/Dialectic** - Correct reasoning
3. **Rhetoric** - Effective speaking and writing

## Renaissance Connection

Medieval rhetoric directly influenced Renaissance humanist education, with figures like:
- Erasmus of Rotterdam
- Rudolf Agricola
- Pietro Bembo
