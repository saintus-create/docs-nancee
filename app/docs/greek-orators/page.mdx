# Greek Orators

> "The roots of rhetoric are in Greece." — George A. Kennedy

## Overview

The Greek orators of classical antiquity established the foundations of rhetorical theory and practice that continue to influence communication today.

## The Ten Attic Orators

### 1. Antiphon (480-411 BC)
- First Athenian logographer (speechwriter)
- Master of forensic rhetoric
- Works on rhetoric and government

### 2. Andocides (440-390 BC)
- Focus on religious and political matters
- Four surviving speeches

### 3. Lysias (445-380 BC)
- Greatest master of simple, elegant prose
- Known for clarity and naturalness
- 34 complete speeches survive

### 4. Isaeus (420-348 BC)
- Specialist in inheritance cases
- Demonstrates logical argument structure

### 5. Isocrates (436-338 BC)
- Founder of rhetorical school
- Panhellenic vision
- Educational treatises
- Famous for periodic style

### 6. Aeschines (389-314 BC)
- Political rival of Demosthenes
- Three surviving speeches

### 7. Demosthenes (384-322 BC)
- Greatest Greek orator
- Champion of Athenian democracy
- "Philippics" against Philip II
- Famous for patriotic speeches

### 8. Hyperides (390-322 BC)
- Leading Athenian statesman
- Eleven speeches partially preserved

### 9. Lycurgus (390-324 BC)
- Administrator and orator
- Reformed Athenian financial system

### 10. Dinarchus (361-291 BC)
- Last of the Attic orators
- Three speeches survive

## Rhetorical Contexts

### 1. Legal (Forensic)
- Courts and litigation
- Accusation and defense

### 2. Political (Deliberative)
- Assembly speeches
- Policy debate

### 3. Ceremonial (Epideictic)
- Funeral orations
- Panegyrics

## Legacy

Greek orators established:
- The five parts of rhetoric
- Styles of delivery
- Methods of argument
- Standards of evidence
