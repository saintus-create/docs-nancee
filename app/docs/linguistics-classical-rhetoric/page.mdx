# Linguistics and Classical Theories of Rhetoric: Connections and Continuity

**Author:** Mark Perkins  
**Publication:** Global Language Review, V(I), 1-9  
**DOI:** 10.31703/glr.2020(V-I).01  
**Year:** 2020

## Abstract

The connections between ancient approaches to rhetoric, as found in Plato and Aristotle, the prime ancient theorists of rhetoric, and modern linguistic approaches to register and genre theory, as in Hallidayan linguistics, show continuity of thought across the centuries. They also suggest that there may be such things as universal rhetorical principles as evidenced in various schemata. However, ethical considerations comprised an essential part of the ancient view of rhetoric. A major feature of the modern age is the opportunity to employ techniques of persuasion by means of new technological channels such as social media and blogs. As the use of these techniques have ethical consequences, so ethical considerations are becoming more prominent and perhaps should be incorporated into linguistic models of register.

## Introduction

A good beginning may be made by comparing the types of questions that the ancients were concerned to answer with those that preoccupy the moderns, and by considering the extent to which ethical questions were seen to be integral to any theory of rhetoric.

### Ancient Questions

The ancients were concerned with quite different sets of questions from those of today. These may be characterised as being of a **philosophical and social** nature, and **formal and technical** nature:

**Philosophical/Social:**
- "What type of argumentation is employed?"
- "What is the relationship of rhetoric with truth?"
- "What is its relationship with ethics?"

**Formal/Technical:**
- "How is an oration to be constructed?"
- "What are the characteristics of good style?"
- "What are the linguistic devices available for attaining that style?"

### Modern Questions

The questions with which the moderns are concerned are:
- "How do the dynamics of discourse (such as a speech) work?" — how is a speech to be analysed
- "How do different components or levels contribute towards the whole?"
- "How is discourse coherent and cohesive?"

Notice that in the case of the how and the why there is a completely different emphasis within modern linguistics. The linguist is concerned with the linguistic how and why, as opposed to the practical purpose of rhetoric — a prominent part of every ancient account.

## Athens and the Beginning of Rhetoric

In the Athens of the 5th century BCE, the Athenian democracy gave all citizens the opportunity of making their voice heard not only in the law courts but also in the deliberative policy-making assemblies. The spoken word became the vehicle of power.

Rhetoric being the art of success, increasing sophistication of presentation led to a demand for education in this sine qua non. Thus, there arose a class of professional men, known as **Sophists**, who travelled from city to city in Greece professing to teach both philosophy and rhetoric.

### The Sophists

The Sophists became notorious for their verbal trickery and argumentation techniques. In Plato's dialogue *Gorgias*, Gorgias distinguishes rhetoric by its capacity to convince by speech any gathering whatever of citizens:

> "I say that it is the ability to persuade with words... and by means of this faculty you will have the doctor as your slave and the athletics trainer!"

Plato thus attributes to Gorgias an unwitting but heavy indictment of rhetoric — there is no check on this power, the skilled orator can simply dictate his will by the power of speech.

## Ancient Theories of Rhetoric

Ancient theories of rhetoric will be found to involve, or at least presuppose in the case of technical handbooks, a standpoint on moral issues.

### Gorgias's Position

Gorgias is also well known as an ontological Philosopher through his treatise *On the Non-existent*. Here he claims that nothing exists; even if something did exist it couldn't be apprehended by man; and if it could be apprehended it would be impossible to communicate it.

This implicitly banishes truth and knowledge from rhetoric. The elements of discourse Gorgias emphasises are:
- The speaker
- Their purpose
- The relevant stylistic devices

### Plato's Position

For Plato, **ethics and epistemology are necessary features** of a satisfactory account of rhetoric. From the *Gorgias* a picture emerges of the Platonic orator as a man who has a knowledge of right and wrong, and who will use this knowledge for the benefit of his audience.

This picture is the inevitable outcome of reasoning from two basic Platonic premises:
1. There are absolute values of right and wrong
2. The person who has knowledge of what is right and wrong will never willingly do evil

## Aristotle's Contribution

Aristotle was a comprehensive theorist and systematiser. His *Ars Rhetorica* provides the first comprehensive statement of rhetorical theory.

### The Four Causes Applied to Rhetoric

| Cause | Application to Rhetoric |
|-------|------------------------|
| **Material** | The raw sounds of discourse |
| **Formal** | The form this discourse takes |
| **Efficient** | The speaker |
| **Final** | The achievement of persuasion |

### The Three Means of Persuasion

Aristotle identifies three principal features of persuasion:

1. **Ethos** — the personal character of the speaker; he should seem a good and trustworthy man
2. **Pathos** — a mode of artistic proof when the souls of the audience are moved to emotion
3. **Logos** — the type of proof found in the argument

### The Three Types of Rhetoric

Aristotle identifies three types of rhetoric according to the types of audience they are addressed to:
- **Judicial** — found in the law courts
- **Epideictic** — involving the praise or blame of a person
- **Deliberative** — found in winning debate

## Text Cohesion and Coherence

In the *Phaedrus*, Socrates criticises a speech on the grounds that it does not cohere. He states:

> "It is necessary for every speech to cohere like a living thing having its own body, so that nothing is lacking in head or foot, but to have a middle and extremities suitable to each other, sketched as part of a whole."

This theory of unity, beginning middle and end, as a necessary condition of coherence, re-appears in Aristotle's *Poetics* and has been influential throughout history.

## Rhetoric and Contemporary Linguistic Approaches

### Halliday's Register Theory

Modern linguistic approaches to rhetoric include Halliday's theory of register, which defines situation in terms of three dimensions:

- **Field** — the subject matter of the text
- **Tenor** — the relationship between author and audience
- **Mode** — the delivery of the text (spoken or written)

### The Proposal

The paper proposes that at the level of discourse, every choice made represents an attempt by the speaker to improve their condition through **persuasion**. A complete and balanced theory would include:

- **Problem-solving** as a major element
- **Feedback** as a dynamic property of text
- **Ethics** in models of text analysis

## Conclusion

The contention is that there is an essential link between **problem-solving and persuasion**, and that these should be manifested in our model of text. The advantage of this model is that it represents text as a realisation of meanings realised in turn by the making of choices and thus the solving of problems.

If persuasion is to be taken into account in such a model of text, it would seem reasonable, if not essential, to include an **ethical framework**. The contemporary term "fake news" indicates disinformation propagated for ethically dubious reasons — there is a long history of fake news stretching from speeches given by orators in the ancient world to the modern era.

New technological channels of communication have facilitated the spread of disinformation, such as social media, and attempts are being made to detect fake news automatically. In that light, ancient approaches to rhetoric are still relevant and provide insight into the ethical role of persuasion in a modern linguistic theory of rhetoric.
