# Encyclopedia of Rhetoric

> "The study of rhetoric is the study of how humans use language to influence one another." — Thomas O. Sloane

## Overview

The *Encyclopedia of Rhetoric* (Arabic: موسوعة البلاغة) is a comprehensive reference work edited by Thomas O. Sloane, first published in 2001 by Oxford University Press. This Arabic translation was published by the National Center for Translation (NCT) in Egypt in 2015.

## Key Concepts

### Ad Hominem Argument (الحجة على الشخص)
A fallacy in which an argument is rebutted by attacking the character, motive, or attribute of the person making the argument, rather than addressing the substance of the argument.

### Allegory (الرمزية)
A literary device in which abstract ideas and principles are described in terms of characters, figures, and events that can be readily understood and visualized.

### Amplification (التكثير)
A rhetorical device that emphasizes and elaborates upon a particular point or idea through the use of emphatic language and expanded detail.

### Anadiplosis (رد العجز على الصدر)
A rhetorical device in which the last word or phrase of one clause is repeated at the beginning of the following clause.

### Anaphora (الاسترجاع)
A rhetorical device that consists of repeating a sequence of words at the beginnings of neighboring clauses, thereby lending them emphasis.

### Anastrophe (التقديم والتأخير)
A literary device in which the normal order of words is inverted for the sake of emphasis, often by moving a preposition or other word to the end of the clause.

### Antithesis (المقابلة)
A rhetorical device in which two opposite ideas are put together in a sentence to achieve a contrasting effect.

### Arrangement (التنظيم)
The organizational component of classical rhetoric, concerned with the logical arrangement of arguments and material.

### Audience (الجمهور)
The recipients of a rhetorical act; the people who receive and interpret messages in the communication process.

### Communication (التواصل)
The process by which information is exchanged between individuals through a common system of symbols, signs, or behavior.
