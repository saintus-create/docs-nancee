# Chinese Rhetoric

> "Chinese rhetoric offers alternative perspectives on persuasion and eloquence." — Xing Lu

## Overview

Chinese rhetorical traditions developed independently from Western rhetoric, offering unique perspectives on language, persuasion, and communication.

## Historical Development

### Classical Period (Warring States, 475-221 BC)
Major philosophical schools developed rhetorical traditions:
- **Confucian** - Proper speech and ritual
- **Mohist** - Universal love and persuasion
- **Daoist** - Natural spontaneity
- **Legalist** - Practical argumentation

### Han Dynasty (206 BC - 220 AD)
- Development of fu (rhyme-prose)
- Historiographical rhetoric

### Tang and Song Dynasties
- Civil service examination system
- Essay writing (八股文)
- Literary criticism

## Key Concepts

### 1. Rectification of Names (正名)
Confucian principle that correct language reflects and maintains social order.

### 2. The Mean (中庸)
Balanced, appropriate expression in speech and conduct.

### 3. Qi (氣)
Vital energy or spirit in rhetorical delivery.

### 4. Wen (文)
Literary refinement and cultural cultivation.

## Rhetorical Practices

### 1. Classical Poetry
- Regulated verse
- Allusion and imagery
- Implicit meaning (言外之意)

### 2. Philosophical Discourse
- Dialogue form
- Parable and example
- Classical allusions

### 3. Historiography
- Model biographies
- Commentary and evaluation
- Moral judgment

## Comparison with Western Rhetoric

| Aspect | Western | Chinese |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Focus | Individual persuasion | Social harmony |
| Goal | Victory in dispute | Moral cultivation |
| Style | Ornamental | Restrained |
| Delivery | Vocal/physical | Inner spirit |
